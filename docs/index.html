Introduction.

As college students, we are interested in learning and understanding the factors that influence student performance. Analyzing the social and economic influences that affect education may allow systemic improvements in academic success and guide future research in this field.

Data curation.

Dataset: Student Performance (Cortez)

Citation: Cortez, Paulo. UCI Machine Learning Repository. Archive.ics.uci.edu, 2014, archive.ics.uci.edu/dataset/320/student+performance.

Description: The dataset(s) were donated to the UC Irvine Machine Learning Repository in 2014. The datasets are about the academic performance of students at two Portuguese secondary education schools called Gabriel Pereira and Mousinho da Silveira. Although this source contains two datasets--one for performance in Mathematics, and the other for performance in Portuguese language--we chose to analyze solely the former because this subject is more generalizable to students like us. Included in the dataset are 649 entries, collected from student responses to questionnaires and information reported by their school records. The attributes are included but not limited to: first period grades, second period grades, final grades, student age, number of class failures, etc. There are 30 features in total, which are explained in more detail below.

Attribute Descriptions # Attributes for both student-mat.csv (Math course) and student-por.csv (Portuguese language course) datasets: 1 school - student's school (binary: 'GP' - Gabriel Pereira or 'MS' - Mousinho da Silveira)

2 sex - student's sex (binary: 'F' - female or 'M' - male)

3 age - student's age (numeric: from 15 to 22)

4 address - student's home address type (binary: 'U' - urban or 'R' - rural)

5 famsize - family size (binary: 'LE3' - less or equal to 3 or 'GT3' - greater than 3)

6 Pstatus - parent's cohabitation status (binary: 'T' - living together or 'A' - apart)

7 Medu - mother's education (numeric: 0 - none, 1 - primary education (4th grade), 2 5th to 9th grade, 3 secondary education or 4 higher education)

8 Fedu - father's education (numeric: 0 - none, 1 - primary education (4th grade), 2 5th to 9th grade, 3 secondary education or 4 higher education)

9 Mjob - mother's job (nominal: 'teacher', 'health' care related, civil 'services' (e.g. administrative or police), 'at_home' or 'other')

10 Fjob - father's job (nominal: 'teacher', 'health' care related, civil 'services' (e.g. administrative or police), 'at_home' or 'other')

11 reason - reason to choose this school (nominal: close to 'home', school 'reputation', 'course' preference or 'other')

12 guardian - student's guardian (nominal: 'mother', 'father' or 'other')

13 traveltime - home to school travel time (numeric: 1 - <15 min., 2 - 15 to 30 min., 3 - 30 min. to 1 hour, or 4 - >1 hour)

14 studytime - weekly study time (numeric: 1 - <2 hours, 2 - 2 to 5 hours, 3 - 5 to 10 hours, or 4 - >10 hours)

15 failures - number of past class failures (numeric: n if 1<=n<3, else 4)

16 schoolsup - extra educational support (binary: yes or no)

17 famsup - family educational support (binary: yes or no)

18 paid - extra paid classes within the course subject (Math or Portuguese) (binary: yes or no)

19 activities - extra-curricular activities (binary: yes or no)

20 nursery - attended nursery school (binary: yes or no)

21 higher - wants to take higher education (binary: yes or no)

22 internet - Internet access at home (binary: yes or no)

23 romantic - with a romantic relationship (binary: yes or no)

24 famrel - quality of family relationships (numeric: from 1 - very bad to 5 - excellent)

25 freetime - free time after school (numeric: from 1 - very low to 5 - very high)

26 goout - going out with friends (numeric: from 1 - very low to 5 - very high)

27 Dalc - workday alcohol consumption (numeric: from 1 - very low to 5 - very high)

28 Walc - weekend alcohol consumption (numeric: from 1 - very low to 5 - very high)

29 health - current health status (numeric: from 1 - very bad to 5 - very good)

30 absences - number of school absences (numeric: from 0 to 93)

Statistical Method 1: Correlation Analysis of First/Second Period Grades and Final Grades

Correlation Analysis We will be examining the relationship between G1 and G2 Grades to the final G3 grades. We hypothesize that high G1 and G2 grades are good predictors of a higher G3 grade. To accomodate for both periods, we will create two hypothesis pairs.

Null Hypothesis 1: G1 grades do not correlate to higher G3 grades Alternative Hypothesis 1: G1 grades are positively correlated to G3 grades Null Hypothesis 2: G2 grades do not correlate to higher G3 grades Alternative Hypothesis 2: G2 grades are positively correlated to G3 grades

We will be computing the correlation value using the Pearson correlation coefficient and comparing the p-value to a significance value of 0.05.

In[5]:

Statistical Method 2: Comparison of Final Grades with/without Family Educational Support

Welch's T-test After splitting the dataset by whether the students have family educational support, we will be comparing the means of final G3 grades. We hypothesize that the expected values of both samples are identical. We will test this at a significance level of 0.05 using Welch's T-test, which is more suitable when comparing samples with unequal sizes and/or variances.

$H_0$: Both samples have identical expected value.

$H_A$: The samples have different expected values.

In[6]:
sig_val = 0.05
with_fam_sup = df[df['famsup'] == 'yes']['G3']
without_fam_sup = df[df['famsup'] == 'no']['G3']

# Two-tailed Welch's T-test
ttest_pval = ttest_ind(with_fam_sup, without_fam_sup, axis=0, equal_var=False)

Statistical Method 3: Comparison of final grades with frequency of going out with friends.

ANOVA Hypothesis Test

$H_0$: The student's frequency of going out with friends does not have an effect on the student's final grade.

$H_A$: The student's frequency of going out with friends has an effect on the student's final grade.

In[8]:
# ANOVA is a test that compares means of multiple groups by looking at their variance

# Define alpha = 0.05; if the p-value is below the alpha or significance value, we will reject the null hypothesis.
sig_val = 0.05

# Group the final grades by the student's response to how frequently they go out with friends. They answered on a scale of 1 to 5.
groups = df.groupby(['goout'])['G3'] # groupby returns a GroupBy object which is like a dictionary

# Extract each group using the .get_group() function on the GroupBy object
one = groups.get_group(1)
two = groups.get_group(2)
three = groups.get_group(3)
four = groups.get_group(4)
five = groups.get_group(5)

# Use the one-way ANOVA function from SciPy and store it in 'res'
res = f_oneway(one, two, three, four, five)

# Use res.pvalue to access the p-value from the test.
print(f'P-value = {res.pvalue} < 0.05 - Reject the null hypothesis. The low p-value indicates evidence that the alternate hypothesis is likely to be true. \nIt\'s likely that the student\'s frequency of going out with friends has an effect on the student\'s final grade.')

P-value = 0.014380078895198904 < 0.05 - Reject the null hypothesis. The low p-value indicates evidence that the alternate hypothesis is likely to be true. 
It's likely that the student's frequency of going out with friends has an effect on the student's final grade.

Primary analysis.

We are ultimately looking at how various factors affect academic performance, as measured by the final (G3) grade. This metric, which we will try to predict, is a continuous variable, meaning that it can take on any value within a range. As such, we will select a machine learning model capable of regression.

Based on our first statistical analysis method, it seems like there is a strong linear correlation at least between the intermediate grades and the final grades, so let's use a linear regression model!

Conclusions

This tutorial sought to explore the factors that go into academic performance in a math class at Portugese secondary schools. In doing so, we hoped to find greater trends in how various demographic, social, and academic features impact students' final grades. These types of insights can inform educators and guide future improvements to curriculums and academic systems.

Exploratory Data Analysis Findings

  1. Our correlation analysis found that first period (G1) and second period (G2) grades were highly positively correlated with final grades (G3). G2 had slightly stronger correlation than G1, indicating that more recent academic performance has a higher impact. This shows the importance of consistent academic performance and ensuring students don't fall behind.
  2. Welch's T-Test compared G3 grades with and without family educational support, which didn't find a statistically significant difference in the average final grades. This could indicate that the precense of family support provides little influence on academic performance, or that representing family support as a binary variable is insufficient.
  3. The ANOVA test showed a statistically significant relationship between the frequence of students going out and their final grades. Tukey's HSD test showed that students who went out less tended to have higher grades than those who did. This shows that excessive social outings could provide a distraction from academics.

Machine Learning Insights

Our primary analysis used a linear regression model to predict final grades based on dataset features. The R^2 score of the trained model is consistently above 0.7 indicating a good fit for the dataset with statistically significant predictive strength.

Extracting the coefficients of the features from the model, we confirm our exploratory data analysis, which found that the most impactful feature by far were G2 scores. This makes intuitive sense that past academic performance predicts future performance.

The reason for the school choice also has a strong impact as those choosing the school because it was close to home had a negative coefficient, perhaps indicating an unacademic motivation. The second-highest coefficient was reason_other, which shows that the survey did not adequately query their responders for their school choice, which could be refined in a future survey.

The jobs of the parents also had very strong coefficient effects depending on the career, with the mother's job having a higher effect. There is also a bias included for which of the two schools the student attends, with Mousinho da Silveira having a positive coefficient, indicating that Mousinho da Silveira provides better education outcomes than Gabriel Pereira.

Social factors, such as a romantic relationship also affects academic performance, as the exploratory data analysis and machine learning results show that the frequency of social outings or the precense of a romantic relationship can negatively affect academics.

Final Thoughts

Our results have shown that there are a lot of contributing factors to academic performance.

We've found that the most consistent keys for success for students is consistent academic performance and a healthy balance between socializing and academics.

Families must also be mindful for how the jobs of parents and the choice of schools can affect the educational outcomes of their child.

Some features could use refinement in future data collection, such as the reason for school choice or family support. More work also needs to be done to replicate survey results in the United States to confirm whether these factors are universal globally.
